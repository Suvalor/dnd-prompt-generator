DND Fantasy Scene Prompt Guide

Guide · last updated June 2026

Scene prompts are the broadest and most complex prompt type. Where a portrait is one face and a token is one silhouette, a scene is a full environment with depth, lighting, atmosphere, and narrative potential. Getting a scene prompt right means your players see the tavern, the dungeon, or the battlefield before you describe it. Getting it wrong means a generic landscape that could be anywhere. This guide covers how to prompt for scenes that feel like places your party could actually visit.

When to use fantasy scene prompts

Use scene prompts when you need an establishing shot or location illustration — a tavern exterior, a dungeon corridor, a mountain pass, a throne room, a forest clearing. Scenes set the stage for encounters; they are the "where" before the "who" and the "what."

Do not use scene prompts when a character is the focus. A dragon perched on a mountain is a monster prompt, not a scene. A tavern with a bard performing is a scene with a secondary character. If the central figure is more important than the environment, use the character or monster prompt type and add a simple background.

The practical rule: if removing all characters from the image would still leave a useful illustration, it is a scene. If removing the characters would leave an empty room, it is a character portrait with a background.

Composition decisions and trade-offs

The most important decision in a scene prompt is the depth layering. A flat scene — "a forest" — produces a pretty picture with no spatial logic. A layered scene — "a forest clearing, fallen log in the foreground, mossy boulders midground, ancient trees fading into mist background" — produces an image with a sense of place and scale. Depth layers are what make a scene prompt produce an illustration you can set an encounter in, rather than a wallpaper you can only look at.

Three-layer rule: Every scene prompt should specify at least three depth layers — foreground detail, midground focal point, and background atmosphere. If you cannot think of three layers, the location is not defined enough to prompt for. Go back to your session notes.

Lighting and time of day is the second critical decision. "A dungeon corridor" looks completely different depending on whether you say "lit by torchlight with warm shadows" or "dimly illuminated by scattered bioluminescent fungi." The time of day also determines the emotional register — dawn conveys hope, dusk conveys closure, midnight conveys danger, noon conveys safety. Never leave lighting unspecified unless you want the model to choose randomly.

Presence of characters is the third axis. A scene with no characters is an establishing shot — pure atmosphere. A scene with tiny silhouetted figures conveys scale and activity without focusing on any individual. A scene with a single prominent figure crosses into "portrait with background" territory. Decide whether characters are present before writing the prompt; adding them as an afterthought usually produces awkward scale mismatches.

Weather and atmosphere is often overlooked but transforms the mood. Rain, fog, snow, smoke, dust — these particles fill the air between the viewer and the scene, adding depth and mood for free. A dry scene reads as clinical; a weather-affected scene reads as lived-in. Always specify at least one atmospheric element.

Common failure patterns and corrections

Failure 1: The empty room. Scene prompts that describe architecture without specifying contents produce images of bare rooms — a stone corridor with no furniture, a tavern with no tables, a forest with no underbrush. The model treats "interior" as a geometry problem, not a storytelling one.
Correction: For every architectural element you describe, add at least one content element. "Stone corridor" becomes "stone corridor with rusted wall sconces and a discarded suit of chainmail on the floor." Content elements tell the model the room is lived-in, not a blueprint.
Failure 2: Flat perspective. When all elements in a scene prompt are given equal weight, the model renders them all at the same apparent depth — a "stacked" image with no receding planes. The result looks like a theater backdrop, not a three-dimensional space.
Correction: Use explicit depth markers. Start with "foreground," then "midground," then "background." Add "receding into the distance" or "vanishing point" to reinforce perspective. The model responds strongly to spatial ordering cues in the prompt.
Failure 3: Mood-word substitution. Writers often substitute abstract mood words for concrete visuals: "ominous" instead of "dark clouds overhead," "serene" instead of "still water reflecting the sky." AI models cannot render abstract moods — they render specific visual features.
Correction: For every mood adjective in your prompt, write the concrete visual it implies. "Ominous" becomes "low clouds, dark water, no visible horizon." "Serene" becomes "flat calm water, pastel sky, soft diffuse light." If you cannot express the mood as a concrete visual, the model cannot render it.

Model-specific guidance

Midjourney

Midjourney is the strongest model for scene art — its painterly style naturally produces atmospheric environments. Use --ar 16:9 or --ar 21:9 for cinematic wide shots. Add --v 6.1 or later for the best spatial coherence. Midjourney's main weakness is text rendering — if your scene includes signs, books, or inscriptions, they will be garbled. Describe text elements as "worn sign with illegible text" to avoid garbled output.

ChatGPT / DALL-E

DALL-E 3 produces cleaner, more geometric scene art — closer to a concept art board than an oil painting. This is ideal for battle maps and architectural reference but less atmospheric for session handouts. DALL-E 3 handles text better than Midjourney but still struggles with more than two or three words. Use DALL-E 3 when you need a clean, readable environment layout; use Midjourney when you need mood and atmosphere.

Stable Diffusion (SDXL / SD3)

Stable Diffusion is the fastest model for scene generation and produces the most detailed output when guided correctly. Structure your prompt with spatial tags: wide shot, environment, [location], foreground [element], midground [element], background [element], [lighting], [weather], [atmosphere], fantasy landscape, highly detailed. Use the negative prompt: character portrait, face, close-up, blurry, low contrast, flat colors. ControlNet with a depth map or edge map can enforce perspective consistency for repeated views of the same location.

Reviewed prompt template

wide establishing shot of [location], [foreground detail with 1-2 specific objects], [midground focal point: the main architectural or natural feature], [background atmosphere: sky, canopy, or distant landscape], [lighting source and quality], [weather or atmospheric element], [character presence: silhouette, small group, or "no characters"], [color palette], fantasy environment illustration, [perspective cue: vanishing point / receding planes / volumetric light], lived-in detail

The template mandates depth layers before style. Character presence is a separate decision, not a default. The "lived-in detail" keyword at the end pushes the model away from empty-room output. As with other templates, order matters — foreground and midground come before atmosphere and style.

Frequently asked questions

How do I show a specific room layout (e.g., for a battle map)?
General image models often struggle to preserve exact grids, room dimensions, and tactical geometry. Use an establishing shot as visual reference, or combine a generated concept with a dedicated map-making tool when precise movement spaces and measurements matter.
Can I generate the same scene from different angles?
Not consistently. AI models do not maintain 3D spatial memory between generations. You can specify a different viewpoint in each prompt ("view from the entrance" vs. "view from the balcony") but the resulting images will not be architecturally consistent. For multi-angle consistency, generate a top-down or isometric view first, then describe specific camera angles that match that layout.
Should I include encounter details (enemies, traps, NPCs) in the scene?
Include encounter elements only if they are meant to be visible to the characters when they enter the scene. A trapped floor that is hidden should not appear in the prompt — it would spoil the encounter. A dragon perched on a hoard that the party will fight should appear because they see it immediately. When in doubt, generate a "pristine" version of the scene and a separate "encounter" version.
Why does my outdoor scene always look like a painting?
Because AI models are trained predominantly on illustrated and painted landscape art. If you want a photographic look, specify "photograph, real location, natural lighting, no stylization" — but be aware that combining photorealism with fantasy elements (floating castles, magical forests) often produces uncanny results. Fantasy scenes benefit from a stylized treatment. Embrace the illustration look.
