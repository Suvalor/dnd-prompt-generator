DND Full-Body Character Prompt Guide

Guide · last updated June 2026

A full-body prompt has a different job from a portrait: it must make the entire character readable at once. Pose, silhouette, armor, weapon, footwear, and the space around the figure all compete for attention. This guide shows how to control those elements without turning the prompt into an inventory list.

When to use a full-body prompt

Choose full-body framing when the character's class fantasy depends on more than their face. A paladin's shield stance, a ranger's layered traveling gear, a monk's balance, or a warlock's staff and familiar all need space below the shoulders. Full-body images also work well for party lineups, campaign handouts, costume references, standees, and character-design sheets.

Use a portrait instead when expression and facial identity matter most. Use a token when the image will sit on a battle grid, and use a scene when the environment or action is the true subject. A full-body image can imply a story, but the character should remain the dominant visual element.

Useful test: if removing the character's boots, stance, weapon, or lower costume would erase an important part of the idea, full-body framing is probably the right choice.

Build the pose before the costume

Start with a pose that can be understood as a silhouette. Strong pose verbs include striding, bracing, guarding, kneeling, drawing a bow, and leaning on a staff. Weak phrases such as "dynamic pose" ask the image model to invent the action and often produce theatrical but unclear anatomy.

Describe the direction of the torso, feet, and main prop. "Standing with weight on the rear leg, shield angled forward, sword lowered at the right side" gives the model a coherent arrangement. Avoid asking both hands to perform unrelated actions unless each action is essential.

Leave visible space around the head, hands, weapon, and feet. Terms such as "complete figure visible from head to toe," "uncropped boots," and "weapon fully inside frame" reduce accidental clipping. A vertical 2:3 or 3:4 composition usually gives a single figure enough room without making the character too small.

Control silhouette and equipment hierarchy

A readable full-body design needs one dominant shape. A broad shield, long cloak, oversized spellbook, curved bow, or pair of horns can carry the silhouette. Pick one hero prop and let secondary equipment support it. Listing a sword, axe, bow, shield, backpack, potion belt, and familiar at equal importance makes the model compete with itself.

Describe clothing from large layers to small details: outer silhouette first, then armor or robe, then belt-level accessories, then footwear. Color should follow the same hierarchy. A useful palette instruction is "deep green cloak over brown leather, with small brass accents" rather than a separate color for every object.

Three-level hierarchy: pose establishes the figure, one hero prop identifies the role, and two or three costume details make the character specific.

Common failure patterns and corrections

Failure 1: Cropped feet or weapons. The model fills the frame with the face and torso, then cuts off whatever reaches the border.
Correction: Request a "complete figure visible from head to toe," name the items that must remain inside frame, and use a vertical aspect ratio with breathing room around the subject.
Failure 2: A stiff catalogue pose. "Standing full body" often produces a symmetrical character facing straight ahead with arms hanging at both sides.
Correction: Use a concrete pose verb and asymmetry: one foot forward, shoulders turned slightly, weight on one leg, or one prop creating a clear diagonal.
Failure 3: Equipment overload. Too many equally emphasized objects lead to duplicated weapons, merged hands, or accessories attached to the wrong side.
Correction: Keep one hero prop, one secondary prop, and two or three costume details. Remove anything that does not change the character's identity at a glance.
Failure 4: The background becomes the subject. A detailed tavern, battlefield, or throne room can shrink the character into a scene.
Correction: Use a plain studio-like backdrop or one restrained environmental cue. Switch to a scene prompt when location and action need equal visual weight.

Model-specific workflow notes

Midjourney

Natural-language pose descriptions and a vertical aspect ratio are a practical starting point. Use --ar 2:3 or --ar 3:4, then vary the result if hands or equipment overlap. --style raw can help when decorative styling overwhelms the requested costume, but results vary by current model version.

ChatGPT image generation / DALL-E

Write the prompt as a clear layout specification: one character, complete head-to-toe framing, named hand positions, and a simple background. If an item keeps being cropped, repeat its spatial requirement in plain language rather than adding more style adjectives.

Stable Diffusion

Put structural tags early, such as full body, standing, head to toe, solo, vertical composition, followed by the character and costume. A pose reference, OpenPose, or another control workflow can improve repeatability when exact limb placement matters. Negative prompts can reduce cropping and duplication, but they cannot replace a coherent positive pose description.

Reviewed prompt template

complete full-body illustration of a [race] [class/role], [specific pose with weight and direction], [hero prop and its position], wearing [outer silhouette layer] over [armor or clothing layer], [two distinctive costume details], [secondary prop or accessory], [footwear fully visible], [hair or head feature], [expression], [simple background], [lighting direction], vertical [2:3 or 3:4] composition, complete figure visible from head to toe, readable fantasy character design

Keep every bracket concrete, but do not force every possible detail into one prompt. Models differ in how they weight prompt order; placing framing, pose, and the hero prop early simply makes the intended hierarchy easier to understand and easier for you to revise.

Frequently asked questions

How do I stop the feet from being cropped?
Use a vertical composition and explicitly request the complete figure, uncropped boots, and space below the feet. Reducing background detail and moving the camera slightly farther back can also help.
Should the character face the camera?
Not always. A slight three-quarter turn often reads more naturally and keeps both the face and costume visible. Front-facing works for a design-sheet look; profile works when the silhouette or a long weapon is the main idea.
Can I show two party members in one full-body prompt?
You can, but consistency becomes harder because the model must track two bodies, outfits, and equipment sets. Generate individual characters first when design accuracy matters, then use a separate party-lineup or scene prompt.
How much background should I include?
One restrained cue is usually enough: a stone floor, misty forest edge, forge wall, or neutral parchment backdrop. If you need architecture, enemies, weather, and action to be equally readable, use a scene prompt instead.
